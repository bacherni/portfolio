# Abstract

This thesis scrutinizes the complex dynamics of conflict across the African continent,
challenging the conventional perspective of homogenous state influence across its territories.
By adopting road infrastructure data as a proxy for state presence, it offers
a nuanced understanding of how variations in state penetration influence conflict dynamics
within nations. The study analyzes conflict events from 1989 to 2022, utilizing
the UCDP Georeferenced Event Dataset, to explore the correlation between the physical
proximity of conflicts to the national capital and their intensity. Findings indicate
that areas with reduced state presence, especially those in remote areas, are more susceptible
to intense conflicts. This observation aligns with Fearon and Laitin’s (2003)
findings about the strategic location choices of rebels in regions with weak state governance.
Distinguishing between state-based, one-sided, and non-state violence, the
research further examines how each conflict type’s spatial location and intensity are
influenced by varying degrees of state penetration. Utilizing both linear and multinomial
logistic regression models, the study highlights the importance of enhancing
state infrastructure and governance in marginalized areas as a deterrent to conflict,
suggesting a path towards more nuanced and effective conflict management strategies.
